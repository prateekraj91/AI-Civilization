# The Chronicle of the Age

*As set down by one hand, looking back on all of it.*

### Before the Written Word

In the beginning the people were few and scattered, and it was the land itself that gathered them: where the soil gave more grain than a household could eat, households stayed, and stored, and became neighbours. Out of that first surplus grew the towns whose names have come down to us — Faircrest, Blackmere, Marrowford, Dunhollow, Duncarrow, and Whitlow — each a cluster of hearths around a common granary. But none of this was written while it happened, for as yet no one could write, and what those first years held we know only as the old people later told it.

It is said the towns did not stay their own for long. Strong men came — some from among the people, some out of the hungry country beyond — and took the granaries by force; and a man who could hold a town and feed a following from it was a king, whatever he had been before. The record knows only that it happened, and more than once. It does not know their names. Their wars and their conquests reach us as rumour, lost to the dark before writing, and here they must stay — a murmur of violence under everything the chronicle can properly tell.

Then, in the second year of the age, the dark lifted. In Blackmere a lettered man the record remembers as Dagmar pressed the first true words into clay — grain-tallies, at first, and then the names of men and the things they had done. It is a small thing, a few marks in mud, and it divided all time in two: everything before it is legend, and everything after it is history. From this year the chronicle can keep faith with names, and the deeds of the kings who came next would not be allowed to fade. It is with the first of them that the true history begins.

## I. The Age of Kings

With writing came kings worth writing of. The age that opened was an age of crowns: Borin, Aldric, and Cyrus, and lesser men who wore the name, each seated above a handful of towns that were his to guard and, more to the purpose, his to tax. A king of those years did not live among his people. He kept his seat apart, with his own larder and his own hired spears, and the towns below sent up to him whatever he asked.

And what they asked was tribute, some of them without limit. Cyrus the Conqueror was the hungriest, though Borin was scarcely gentler. Season upon season the levies climbed from the granaries to the royal seats — grain to feed soldiers, coin to hire more — and season upon season the towns that grew the grain kept a little less of it. It bought the kings their wars. It was also, though no one yet dared say so, the first cause of everything that would in time break them.

It was Borin the Grasping who struck first. In the fourth year, with a war chest his towns had filled, he fell upon the kingdom of Aldric and broke it. Aldric's crown passed to him, and for the first time one man held more than his own country. This was the beginning of empire in the land — and, though it wore the face of a triumph, the beginning of the end of kings.

For a crown taken by force is a crown that must be held by force, and holding it cost more tribute, and more tribute cost the towns more of what little the levies had left them. The wider a king reached, the harder he had to squeeze — and the towns were counting every season of it. What the kings had built to make themselves great was already, quietly, teaching their people to hate them.

## II. The Empire and Its Breaking

The years that followed were years of one crown swallowing another, and then failing to keep it down. The land was too wide and the loyalties too thin for any single throne to hold what its armies could take.

The strongest of the kings did not stay strongest: in the twenty-third year Cyrus the Conqueror brought Borin down in his turn. Each conqueror became, in his hour, the next man's prize, and the crown of empire never sat long on any one head.

But it was not war that undid the empires so much as sheer distance. A realm seized is a realm that strains to be free, and one after another the subject kings pulled loose. Cyrus was the first to go, in the sixth year; and Borin took the same road after him, each preferring his own small crown to a seat beneath a greater one. No sooner was an empire forged than it began to shed its provinces, and the grand conquests of the age dissolved almost as fast as they were won.

So the age of great crowns spent itself: the kings made war to grow, and taxed to make war, and the growing would not hold and the taxing would not stop. The empires broke along their own seams. But the deeper break was not between king and king. It was building, all this while, between the kings and the towns that fed them — and it had not yet been paid.

## III. The Risings

And in the end it was paid. The towns had grown the grain and sent it up and kept less of it each year, and there is a limit to what a people will surrender to a seat they never chose. When the breaking came at last it did not come from a rival king. It came from below.

In Whitlow the people rose against Garrick and pulled him from his seat, and Merrick the Liberator was lifted up in his place to hold it in the people's name.

Dunhollow was next. Its people cast Edric out, and Orla the Liberator came up out of the crowd to take the vacant seat.

In Blackmere, too, the grievance boiled over: Dagmar was thrown down, and it was Corvin the Liberator the people raised to the seat he had held. There was a bitter justice in it: Dagmar had given his people their first letters, and they used the memory those letters kept to remember every grain he had taken from them.

But the hardest fall was a king's. Borin the Grasping, who had won his crown in war and had taxed his towns to the bone to pay for the winning, came in the thirty-fifth year to the end every grasping crown earns. It was not a rival army that unseated him. It was Marrowford, the very town whose granaries had filled his war chest: it rose beneath him and would give no more, and Neris the Liberator led it. The hungriest crown of the age ended as it had fed — torn from him by the same hands that had fed it.

Not every rising won. In Duncarrow the people rose and were put down, and the seat held a while longer over the bodies of those who had dared. But a throne that must be defended against its own town is a throne already lost, whatever the tally of one season's dead.

When the dust settled, one crown alone was still upright. Cyrus the Conqueror had outlived every rival and outlasted every revolt, and sat his throne at the close of the age as though the whole convulsion had been arranged to leave him there. Whether that was strength or only luck, the chronicle does not presume to say.

So closes the age this chronicle can tell. It opened with a surplus of grain and it ended with a surplus of grief, and the road between the two ran straight: the surplus made lords, and the lords made tribute, and the tribute made rage, and the rage made revolt. What the risings will make in their turn, another hand must set down.
