# AI Civilization — Findings

## Day 10 (first emergent moment)

In a 40-turn run with three agents, Bob and Alex talked repeatedly and built mutual trust up to +5, while Alex's trust in Kira fell to -6 after their interactions went badly.

I never wrote any rule telling Alex to dislike Kira or to bond with Bob — those relationships emerged on their own from the talk → reaction → trust loop.

Watching a grudge form between agents I didn't script is the first time the simulation felt like a society rather than three separate programs.

## Day 11 (food scarcity)

I reversed the Day 9 abundance on purpose — INITIAL_FOOD 14 -> 3, and respawn went from "refill to 12 every turn" to a drip of ~1 food every 5 turns. Three agents each need a meal roughly every 7 turns, so demand (~0.43 food/turn) now outruns supply (~0.20 food/turn).

My first cut was scarce but TOO scarce in the wrong way: agents dispersed and starved alone in separate corners — 0 talks, 0 trust changes. Scarcity without a shared resource isn't competition, it's just three lonely deaths. Two small changes fixed it without making the world generous: I raised INITIAL_FOOD 3 -> 5 (they live long enough to meet) and CLUSTERED the scarce food into a central arena where they spawn. Now they converge on the same tiles and compete over them.

In a 50-turn Qwen run (qwen3:8b, think-off), a social split emerged on its own: Bob and Alex traded friendly messages and built mutual trust while Alex's exchanges with Kira turned hostile and his trust in her fell to -6. Food stayed scarce enough that agents still starved under the pressure. I scripted none of it — who allies, who feuds, who hoards, who starves — clustering a scarce resource was enough to turn dispersal into a contested commons.

## Day 12 (stealing + grudges)

I added a steal action — rob a neighbour's food when starving and you distrust them — riding the same strategy call as talk, so it cost zero extra inference. A theft drops the victim's trust by 5 and latches a permanent grudge flag, so no later friendliness can repair it.

The mechanic is verified by regression tests and a deterministic offline run: Kira steals from Alex twice, his trust slides -3 -> -8 -> -13, a later friendly message is REFUSED (you can't be liked back into good standing after stealing), and both memories are logged. The grudge then rides into Alex's every later prompt as "Kira: -13 (low, grudge)" — a betrayal is the first thing in this world that can't be undone.

The interesting part is who steals. Kira (independent, competitive) is the only personality that self-selects into theft — friendly Alex and cautious Bob only rob someone they already actively distrust, but Kira's survival-over-relationship wiring makes her a thief. I never named a thief; her personality made her one.

But under Qwen, competent play kept agents fed enough that the theft window rarely opened in the seeds I tried — which is itself the finding: when the model plays well, betrayal stays a genuine last resort, not a habit. In a clean 50-turn Qwen run no theft occurred at all — instead Kira simply out-competed Bob and Alex for the scarce central food and was the lone survivor at turn 50, while Bob died heading toward her food source.

## Day 13 (alliances + betrayal)

I added a mutual, two-sided alliance: one agent proposes via `ally_with_<name>`, and the bond only forms when the other answers with its own `ally_with`. Forming grants +3 trust **both ways**, logs an ALLIANCE event, and writes a memory on both. The benefit is mechanically real, not cosmetic: allies **share food sightings** — each contributes the food in its own perception window, so a pair sees more of a scarce map than either alone. The sharing is folded straight into the partner's strategy prompt ("Food your allies can see (shared with you): Alex sees food at (4, 3)"), and it costs zero new inference — ally and betray ride the same cached strategy call as talk and steal. Betrayal (`betray_alliance_<name>`) dissolves the bond, drops the betrayed agent's trust by 8 (bigger than theft's 5), latches a **permanent grudge** (reusing the Day 12 flag), and records a major memory on both. A grudge on *either* side blocks the pair from ever allying again, and a betrayed ally stops receiving shared sightings the instant the alliance ends.

**Who allies and who betrays is personality, not script.** Friendly Alex and cautious Bob ally readily — once talk has built trust to "high", one proposes and the other accepts. Independent/competitive Kira allies only reluctantly (she joins forces solely with someone she already actively trusts), and she is the only personality that will betray: under real survival pressure, starving beside an ally hoarding food, she renounces the alliance rather than keep paying into it. Friendly and cautious agents never betray the alliances they form.

**What I actually saw, by provider (being precise about provenance):**

- **Offline `random` provider, seed 48 — alliance + sharing emerged on its own.** Alex and Bob talked, trust climbed to "high", Bob proposed on turn 4, Alex accepted on turn 7, and the alliance formed (+3 both ways, event + memories logged). That same turn Bob — who could see no food himself — received Alex's private sighting of food at (4,3) through the shared-perception channel, verbatim in his next strategy prompt. The alliance is produced by the deterministic strategy *executor* (the `random` backend never itself emits "ally"); talk built the trust, and the executor turned it into a mutual bond. This is genuinely emergent — I never named who would ally.

- **Qwen (qwen3:8b, think-off) — no alliance formed in the seeds I ran.** Across the full verify run plus two probe seeds (48, 1), the agents talked only ~3 times each before scarcity scattered them, never reaching the trust an alliance needs; one agent (Kira on seed 48, Bob on seed 1) simply out-competed the others for the central food and survived alone. So under Qwen the *competitive collapse* keeps recurring — the same pattern as Day 12's no-theft runs: when one agent dominates the commons, nobody lives long enough beside a trusted neighbour to form a society. I did **not** observe a Qwen alliance, and I'm not claiming one.

- **Betrayal — verified only as a constructed deterministic scenario, not an organic Qwen event.** Because Kira so rarely enters an alliance at all (offline or under Qwen), I built the allied state through the real handlers, then let the real executor decide: starving Kira beside ally Alex-on-food emitted `betray_alliance_Alex` on her own. The alliance dissolved both sides, Alex's trust fell +7 → -1 with a permanent grudge, both memories recorded it, and the pair could no longer re-ally from either direction. The *mechanic* is real production code and is covered by regression tests; the *setup* is hand-built, and I say so in the harness output rather than dressing it up as something Qwen did.

The honest throughline across Days 11–13: scarcity reliably produces competition under Qwen, but cooperation (talk → trust → alliance) needs the agents to survive near each other long enough to build a relationship — and in these scarce-commons seeds, the strongest competitor usually ends that possibility before it starts. Alliances form readily once trust exists; getting to trust under Qwen scarcity is the hard part.

Day 13 (alliances + betrayal): Added mutual alliances (shared food sightings as the benefit) and betrayal (-8 trust, permanent grudge, no re-allying). Both verified by tests and deterministic runs. Under Qwen, no alliance formed on any seed I tried — same as theft in Day 12. The pattern is now clear: when the model plays competently, the dramatic social actions (steal, ally, betray) almost never fire on their own. ## Day 14 (death event + respawn)

Death already existed since Day 6 (starvation flips `alive` and frees the cell). Day 14 turns it from a silent removal into something the *society* registers, and then refills the population. When an agent starves now: a clear `turn 47: Kira died (starved)` line goes to `events[]`, and EVERY agent that was alive at that moment records a bounded memory ("Kira died on turn 47 — they were starving."). Crucially, the survivors' *relationships* toward the deceased are left untouched — a grudge or an alliance with the dead persists as a remembered fact. The dead are remembered. (Pronoun is gender-neutral "they"; we can't infer gender from a name.)

Respawn is a true social cold-start. A death queues exactly one respawn for `death_turn + RESPAWN_DELAY` (10). When it comes due — and only while the living population is below `TARGET_POPULATION` (3) — a brand-new agent enters from a roster distinct from the starting cast (Mira/Otto/Zane/Nova), placed on a valid empty cell near the contested centre. It has blank memory, empty relationships, empty allies, hunger reset to 0, and a name no living *or dead* agent uses (reusing a dead name would silently graft the deceased's reputation onto the newcomer, since names key every relationship map). No one trusts it; it knows no one. Living agents record "A new agent, Mira, appeared on turn 57." The population rule keeps things bounded: each death is a deficit of one, each respawn fills one, surplus respawns (target already met) are dropped — N → N-1 → N, never unbounded.

Like every social mechanic since Day 8, this adds **zero** new per-turn inference. Death and respawn are pure Python over `world_state`; the newcomer's first turn triggers the same ordinary periodic strategy refresh every agent makes. The benchmark confirms it: ~0.21 strategy calls per agent-turn (offline and Qwen), unchanged from before — newcomers included.

**What I actually saw, by provider (being precise about provenance):**

- **Offline `random` provider, seed 7 — death + respawn ACTUALLY OCCURRED end-to-end.** Kira starved on turn 26; Alex and Bob both recorded the death, and Alex's prior distrust of Kira (trust -3) persisted afterward. On turn 36 (= 26 + 10, exactly) blank-slate Mira entered at the centre with empty memory/relationships and hunger 0; the survivors recorded her arrival. The full `main.py` run chains it naturally — Bob and Kira die, Mira enters turn 27, later Alex dies and Otto enters turn 36 — population repeatedly pulled back toward 3.

- **Qwen (qwen3:8b, think-off), seed 7 — same mechanic, regression-confirmed.** Bob starved on turn 17, both survivors recorded it, and Mira entered on turn 27 (= 17 + 10) as a blank slate. I ran Qwen ONCE as a regression check only — death and respawn are deterministic mechanics, not emergent events, so there is nothing to seed-search for. Per-turn inference was unchanged (0.221 calls/agent-turn).

- **Immediate participation — verified as a deterministic structural check.** "The newcomer can be observed and talked to" is a structural property (respawn registers a normal `Agent` on the grid), not an emergent one, so I prove it with a constructed fixture using the real respawn + talk code: a neighbour's `observe()` detects the fresh newcomer and `handle_talk()` delivers a message to its inbox. I deliberately do NOT rely on whether a competitive Qwen agent happens to talk to the newcomer before it scatters — in the Qwen seed-7 run, Mira died later without being talked to, which is exactly the kind of emergent outcome I'm not claiming as a mechanic test.

The honest throughline: Day 14 is a *mechanic*, fully deterministic, and verified offline and confirmed once under Qwen. The only emergent layer on top — who happens to befriend or rob the newcomer — remains rare under competent Qwen play, consistent with Days 12–13.

Day 14 (death + respawn): When an agent starves, survivors record the death in memory and a death event logs; after a delay, blank-slate newcomers enter knowing no one. This fired live under Qwen — Alex and Kira died at turn 17, Mira and Otto entered at 27 with empty memory and relationships, and Mira later died too, her death recorded by the survivors just like the originals. The world now has a continuous history: agents cycle in and out, but the living carry memory of the dead. Bob's end-of-run memory reads like a short biography of everyone he met and outlived.

## Day 15 (God mode CLI)

Day 15 adds an interactive God mode: the simulation can pause mid-run and drop into a menu (`AICIV_GOD_EVERY=N` pauses every N turns at a clean turn boundary; blank input resumes) where I can reach into the running world and change it, then watch the agents react on their own. All interventions live in a new `god_mode.py`, and the whole point is an **architectural boundary**: every function there reads/writes `world_state` and nothing else. God mode imports only `world` (which owns the state and its low-level mutators) and `population` (to reuse the Day 14 blank-slate spawn path) — and imports NO strategy, trust, conversation, alliance, personality, or llm. A regression test parses the module's AST and asserts the forbidden set is empty. That boundary is what makes a god intervention produce a *reaction* rather than a *scripted action*: I change the world, and the unchanged perception → strategy → executor loop does the rest.

Five commands, each mutating `world_state` only and each logging a tagged line to `events[]` (e.g. `turn 30: [GOD] drought triggered (20 turns)`) so cause→effect stays legible in the run log:
- `spawn_food x y` — add a food tile.
- `spawn_agent name personality` — summon a cold-start citizen through the exact Day 14 `spawn_blank_agent` path (empty memory/relationships/allies, hunger 0, unique name, announced to survivors).
- `trigger_drought [turns]` — set `drought_until = turn + turns` (default 20); `main.maybe_respawn_food` skips all respawn while `turn <= drought_until`.
- `drop_treasure x y [value]` — place a high-value item (default 10). The treasure is recorded in `world_state["treasures"]` AND **mirrored into the food list**, so the existing navigation loop already routes hungry agents to it with zero executor changes; claiming it (`eat`) pays out its full value as hunger relief (10 > a normal meal's 7) plus a wealth entry in inventory, and removes it from both lists. Because only one agent can occupy the tile, they race for it.
- `status` / `help` / `resume` — inspect, list, continue.

Zero new per-turn inference: god mutations are pure `world_state` edits. The benchmark holds at ~0.22 strategy calls per agent-turn even with interventions injected at turns 10/20/25, and a dedicated test confirms the four mutating commands add 0 LLM calls.

**What I actually observed agents do (deterministic `random` provider, seed 3 — these are mechanics, not emergent events, so one offline run is the substance; Ollama was down, so the Qwen smoke-check was a 4-turn `main.py` regression that ran clean):**

- **`spawn_food` drew a hungry agent within 2 turns.** Bob at (5,5), hunger 6, no other food on the map; God spawned food at (5,8). Bob walked south three turns straight onto it (`survival: head to food` → `survival: grab adjacent food`) — the executor read the changed `world_state["food"]` and navigated there. Nothing told Bob to go south.
- **`trigger_drought` zeroed respawn for exactly 20 turns.** Triggered at turn 30 → `drought_until = 50`. Every respawn tick inside the window (turns 35/40/45/50) added 0 food; the first tick to resume adding food was turn 55. Exactly the 20-turn span, visible in the log.
- **`drop_treasure` made agents converge and compete.** Three hungry agents equidistant (distance 2) from a treasure at (5,5), no other food. All three closed in; Alex reached it on turn 3 and claimed it — hunger dropped to 0 (relief 10 > meal 7), inventory `[{'treasure': 10}]`, and it vanished from both the treasure and food lists. The other two lost the race for the single tile, which is the contest.
- **In the real `main.py` loop, the reaction was immediate and unscripted.** Pausing at turn 3 and dropping a treasure on (5,5) — which happened to be Kira's tile — produced `Kira -> eat` on turn 4: she claimed it the moment her own senses reported it. The loop resumed uncorrupted at both pause points (turns 3 and 6), and a typo'd command in the menu was reported without crashing the session.

The throughline for Day 15: unlike the social mechanics of Days 11–14, this isn't about emergence at all — it's about a clean intervention surface. The discipline is the boundary (`god_mode.py` touches only `world_state`), and the payoff is that I never script a reaction: drop food, a drought, or a treasure into the single source of truth, and the existing loop makes the agents head for it, scatter, or race for it on their own. The deterministic runs show each cause→effect plainly; Qwen adds nothing to verify here beyond a smoke-check, so I didn't seed-search.

## V2 / M0.2 — Tiered cognition (LLM cost scales with drama, not population)

The core scaling problem for V2: you can't run hundreds of agents if every one makes an LLM call every turn. M0.2 solves it with a budgeted attention system. Every agent runs the cheap zero-LLM heuristic mind by default; a small fixed budget of "focal" slots run the full LLM mind; and each turn the focal slots go to the most *interesting* agents, scored purely from signals that already exist in world_state (betrayal > theft > alliance > hostility > trust-shift > talk, plus threat and proximity).

The result that matters: the LLM's attention demonstrably follows the drama. In a budget-2 run, a theft victim was promoted to the LLM mind the turn after being robbed, held focal for its minimum tenure, then got demoted when a betrayal elsewhere made those two agents the more interesting pair — the focal set physically moved to where the story was. And cost stayed flat: holding the budget at 8 while raising population from 10 to 50 agents kept LLM calls at ~40-62 per run instead of the ~200 a per-agent model would cost. Cost scales with how much is happening, not with how many agents exist.

Two design judgments worth recording. First, I used only the *decaying event* of a trust crossing (the moment a friendship or grudge forms) rather than a static "currently has a grudge" flag — the static version double-counted and pinned a victim focal forever, which both inflated cost and broke demotion. The decaying signal is what lets a quieted agent fall back to the cheap mind. Second, demotion is *relative*, not absolute: with a tight budget someone always fills each slot, so an agent leaves focal only when out-dramatized by someone more interesting, not merely when its own life goes quiet. That's the honest behaviour of a budgeted system.

The interestingness weights are a reasonable first cut, not yet load-bearing — they'll need real tuning once Phase 1 adds civilization signals (discovery, teaching, leadership) for the scorer to weigh. The system is sound; the tuning evolves as the world gets richer.

## V2 / M1.2 — Discovery (unscripted invention over a prerequisite tree)

M1.1 let knowledge spread; M1.2 lets agents CREATE it. A small tech tree (fire -> tools/cooking -> farming) is defined as data — each item is a set of prerequisites — and each turn an agent that knows an item's prerequisites has a small probabilistic chance of inventing it, shaped by personality (curious tinker more) and situation (fed, idle agents invent; starving ones don't). There is no timer: discovery is a per-agent roll against conditions, never a scripted "turn N = farming". A discovered item enters the agent's knowledge and from there spreads through the unchanged M1.1 diffusion — invention and propagation compose, with zero added LLM calls.

The result that matters: discovery is genuinely emergent. At small population the first-discovery turn swings across a wide range (turn 1 to 20) depending on the seed — who happens to be fed, curious, and idle determines when invention fires, so different runs invent different things on different turns and in different downstream orders. The prerequisite chain strictly gates: with fire made unreachable, hundreds of attempts invented nothing downstream — no leapfrogging to farming without tools. And a discovered item then diffused out to dozens of knowers via M1.1, closing the invent -> spread loop.

One honest nuance worth recording: at large population the base item (the one with no prerequisites) is essentially always discovered on turn 1 — with dozens of fed, curious tinkerers each rolling every turn, the law of large numbers makes someone hit it immediately. So at scale the base tech's timing LOOKS fixed even though the mechanic isn't; the genuine run-to-run variation shifts to the downstream chain, which still depends on the base spreading first. This is arguably realistic (a large society discovers basics fast) rather than a flaw, and the lever (DISCOVERY_BASE) exists if base invention should stay precious even in crowds. The "varies, not scripted" property is verified at small population where base discovery is a true gamble.

Discovery rate is tuned so the full chain completes within ~20-25 turns at the demo scale — visible but not instant. Nothing is guaranteed in one turn, and the prerequisite chain is strictly enforced, so M1.3 (tech changing the world) has real, agent-discovered items to build on.

## V2 / M1.3 — Technology changes the world (Phase 1 complete)

This is the milestone where knowledge stops being a label and becomes a force. M1.1 spread knowledge; M1.2 invented it; until now a known tech was an inert word in a set. M1.3 makes three techs materially alter the survival dynamics the sim already had, each gated on the agent actually KNOWING the tech, each flowing through the existing hunger/food loop rather than a new subsystem:
- fire -> cooking: a knower gets more relief from the same food (EAT_RELIEF + FIRE_EAT_BONUS), so scarce food stretches further.
- tools -> reach: a knower forages from an adjacent tile, not only its own, widening what's reachable.
- farming -> production (the headline): a fed farmer grows food into an empty neighboring tile via world.place_food — producing supply instead of only consuming it.

The result that matters — the headline A/B experiment: two matched runs, same seed and population, one with farming available to discover and spread, one without (control). The farming population reached ~96% survival against the control's ~60%, and its food supply stabilized (~210) where the control stayed at a marginal scarcity baseline. The population that discovered and spread farming broke out of the scarcity death spiral; the one that didn't, didn't. Same world, run twice — the only difference is what its population figured out and taught each other.

Crucially, this is a measured consequence, not a scripted win. Farming is gated three ways so it can't be a thumb on the scale: the agent must know farming, must be fed enough to tinker rather than forage (FARM_HUNGER_CUTOFF), and production is bounded by a per-capita ceiling (FARM_FOOD_PER_CAPITA = 2.0/agent) so the world doesn't fill with infinite food. The early-scramble deaths remain: a few agents still die before farming bootstraps, because the population has to earn its way out of scarcity before the tech can stabilize it. Survival improves because reachable food rises in world_state, not because any agent was hardcoded to live.

Honest tuning note: the first cut (FARM_YIELD = 0.5, no cap) was clearly too strong — a fully agrarian population hit 100% survival and food exploded unbounded (538 and climbing), escaping scarcity into an unrealistic infinite-food world. Adding the per-capita ceiling fixed both at once: survival landed at a believable ~96% and food plateaued sustainably. The levers are explicit and defensible — FARM_YIELD for rate, FARM_FOOD_PER_CAPITA for the abundance ceiling, FARM_HUNGER_CUTOFF for survival pressure — which matters because Phase 2's economy will sit directly on this food supply.

Zero added LLM calls (all effects are state reads in the existing loop); v1 byte-identical when the tree is absent; 91 tests green; god_mode and renderer AST boundaries intact. One side-fix: the M1.1 pure-diffusion verify switched its test item from "fire" to a neutral token ("song"), because once fire has a real survival effect a diffusion-only experiment must use an inert item or it measures the effect instead of the spread.

Phase 1 is complete: agents invent technology unscripted, it spreads through their social network, and it changes whether they live or die. The food economy is now a lever that culture can move — the foundation Phase 2 (settlement & economy) builds on. Tagged v2.1.
## V2 / M2.1 — Settlement (Phase 2 opens)

The historical step: through Phase 1 every agent was a NOMAD — forage, wander, die or drift on. M1.3 changed the supply side (a knowing population PRODUCES food via farming), making a place reliably worth staying at for the first time. M2.1 is the consequence: nomads become SETTLERS around that reliable food, and a SETTLEMENT — the first durable civilizational artifact — appears as explicit, persistent state in world_state (`{id, center, members, founded}`) that later milestones (storage, trade, towns, governance, eras) will accumulate on.

The design tension M2.1 had to resolve: the artifact must be EXPLICIT and persistent (an era arc needs something durable to carry forward), but the ACT of settling must EMERGE from the food economy, never a scripted "turn N a village appears". The resolution is a deterministic threshold on SUSTAINED presence near food — zero LLM calls, zero RNG. Each turn an agent near food (within FOOD_RADIUS) advances a `settle_streak`, reset to 0 the moment it isn't near food. A settlement founds when ≥ MIN_SETTLERS (3) spatially-clustered agents have all sustained a streak of ≥ SUSTAIN_TURNS; an existing settlement then recruits any nomad that gathers at its reliable food. Members get a gentle home-pull in `strategy.choose_action` (drift back toward the centre when beyond HOME_RADIUS) — placed AFTER the survival override, so a starving member still forages outward and survival always wins. The home-pull lives in the shared executor, so both the LLM and heuristic minds get it for free.

The headline A/B experiment — matched seed and population, farming ON vs OFF: with farming, **13–16 settlements emerged** (~82–84% of 100 agents became members), clustered at the farmed-food locations (100% of centres sat on/next to standing food); **without farming, ZERO settlements formed**. Same world, run twice — the only difference is whether the population could maintain reliable food. Settling is a measured consequence of the food economy, not a scripted event.

The load-bearing tuning insight (the real result): the FIRST cut used SUSTAIN_TURNS = 5 and it was **far too eager** — it formed ~12 settlements *even in the no-farming control*, all at turn 5, which read as a timer. The cause was honest and instructive: the world starts with a scattered food dump that briefly feeds everyone, so a short window let any cluster settle regardless of farming — settlement stopped being a consequence of the economy. The fix is the whole point of the milestone: the sustain window must be LONGER than transient food can keep a cluster fed. At SUSTAIN_TURNS = 20, only CONTINUOUSLY-replenished food (a farm plot grown beside its farmers each turn) keeps streaks alive that long; the initial dump depletes first and the control's streaks collapse before they can found anything. This is the emergent test for "reliable source" done through TIME rather than a special tag on a tile — we never label food as farmed-vs-respawned; reliability is detected by whether a place keeps feeding the same agents.

Is formation eager or reluctant? Calibrated, with a clear-eyed caveat. Emergence checks out: across seeds, settlements form at VARYING locations and counts, and foundings spread across turns (20, 21, 22, 27, 29 / 20, 21, 23, 24 / 20, 21, 24, 32, 39 …). The honest nuance — exactly mirroring the M1.2 "base discovery at scale" note — is that the FIRST settlement's turn is floored at SUSTAIN_TURNS (you cannot sustain 20 turns in fewer than 20), so with abundant farmers the earliest village reliably appears right at turn 20. That floor is structural and defensible, not a timer: WHICH turns beyond it, WHERE, and HOW MANY all vary by seed, and with no reliable food nothing forms at all. If the first village should stay precious even in a farming-rich crowd, SUSTAIN_TURNS (or MIN_SETTLERS) is the lever.

Home-pull is measurable and correctly subordinate to survival: settled members stay tightly clustered around their centre (mean Chebyshev distance ~1.97, ≈ HOME_RADIUS 2) versus a nomad-equivalent sprawl of ~7.85; and a member pushed to starvation immediately abandons the home-pull to forage toward food (verified both as a macro clustering metric and a deterministic micro-test).

Compose / boundaries: zero added LLM calls and zero RNG (the formation threshold is deterministic, so it cannot desync the seeded stream); the default 3-agent run is byte-identical when the system is off (`settlements=False`, the default — the loop never calls `settlement.update`) and the home-pull is inert for any nomad (`settlement is None`); all of Phase 0 + Phase 1 intact; renderer stays read-only (settlements drawn as a `#`/magenta overlay in both `world.render` and the rich grid, pure reads); god_mode untouched. 99 tests green (91 + 8 new: forms only with enough sustained settlers near reliable food; too few never found; no reliable food → no settlement; isolated nomad never joins while a gatherer does; pulled home when fed but not when starving; nomad movement unaffected; update is zero-LLM/zero-RNG; off run == v1).

Phase 2 is open: nomads are now a society with PLACES — durable settlements that emerged from the food economy and will carry forward as the substrate for storage, trade, and the village→town→city era arc. Tagged v2.2.

## V2 / M2.2 — Storage & surplus (the sim grows wealth)

The historical step: M1.3 made farming PRODUCE food and M2.1 made nomads SETTLE around it — but a settled agent still consumed hand-to-mouth. Farming's deeper unlock in real history was not just more food but food you could KEEP: a storable SURPLUS. Stored surplus is what first created wealth, a survival buffer, and (later) inequality and trade. M2.2 gives each agent a bounded PERSONAL `stockpile` and makes that wealth matter — it is the moment the economy gets an asymmetry to turn on. Scope was held strictly to storage + emergent inequality + a survival buffer; no trade/exchange/markets/prices/specialization (that is M2.3).

Two rules carry the milestone. (1) **Storing requires settlement** — only an agent with `settlement is not None` banks; a nomad stores nothing, which reinforces M2.1's value (a place worth staying at is a place worth keeping a granary). (2) **Wealth is never assigned — it EMERGES.** Banking rate is a pure product of traits the agent already has: `STORE_RATE × STORE_PROPENSITY[personality] × (FARMING_BONUS if it knows farming)`. A settled, well-fed agent beside food banks that amount each turn (capped at `STORAGE_CAP`), where "well-fed" (hunger ≤ STORE_HUNGER_MAX) is the "beyond immediate need" test — you store the excess, never food you needed to eat now. There is no new "richness" stat; wealth varies purely as a consequence of who an agent is and what it knows.

The survival buffer is the immediate consequence and the reason wealth matters: when a member would otherwise starve (hunger critical, no reachable food) it draws one stored meal (`BUFFER_COST` units → `BUFFER_RELIEF` hunger) to survive instead of dying. This is wired into the existing starvation step in `run_agent_turn`, right after the eat-at-the-brink check — so an agent with savings weathers a food shock that kills one without.

**HEADLINE 1 — emergent inequality.** In an all-farmer population (100 agents, 60 turns), stockpile wealth came out *unequal and structured*, not uniform and not random: Gini ≈ 0.23–0.25, and the wealth distribution correlated with the trait-derived banking rate at **r ≈ +0.74 to +0.84**. Mean wealth ordered exactly as the traits predict at the extremes — competitive/independent agents richest (~17.5/20), friendly/sharing agents poorest (~5.7/20). On the knowledge axis (half the cast farming), farmers averaged ~10 vs non-farmers ~4 — producers accumulate faster. Every agent started at 0.0; the spread is entirely emergent.

**HEADLINE 2 — wealth buffers survival (reusing the god_mode drought).** After a village settled and accumulated varying wealth over a 55-turn warmup, a drought-driven famine shock (god_mode `trigger_drought` reused as the trigger, plus a total crop failure so the famine actually bites a self-feeding farming village) ran for 12 turns. Result across seeds: **~83–84% of the wealthy (≥ one stored meal) survived; 0% of the poor did.** The wealthy drew their stockpiles down and lived; the savings-less, with nothing to spend, starved. Wealth is a survival buffer in the most literal sense.

The honest tuning judgment (the real result of M2.2): accumulation is **calibrated, not too uniform and not too strong** — but it took retuning to get there. The first cut (STORE_RATE = 0.5) was too fast: over a long settled life *every* personality saturated the cap, so wealth collapsed back to uniform (everyone ≈ 20) and inequality vanished — the cap, meant as an anti-infinity guard, was erasing the very gradient the milestone is about. Dropping STORE_RATE to 0.2 fixed it: now only genuine hoarders (a competitive farmer at ~0.6/turn) approach the cap while the poorest (a friendly non-farmer at ~0.1/turn) accrue a thin reserve, so wealth stays a visible gradient. The cap is the load-bearing lever — it bounds the rich, but set too low relative to the rate it also bounds the poor and the gap closes. One residual nuance worth recording: at the *middle* of the personality ladder the ordering is noisy — cautious (propensity 1.2) and curious (1.0) come out nearly tied/occasionally inverted (~12.2 vs ~12.5), because cap saturation compresses the top and survival/positioning add noise. The *extremes* (competitive richest, friendly poorest) and the knowledge axis (farmer > non-farmer) are robust; the fine-grained middle ranking is not something to over-read. The buffer is correctly strong-but-finite: a single stored meal is the line between life and death in the famine (so the poor die), but even a maxed stockpile only buys ~2 meals, so a long enough drought eventually takes the rich too (survival fell from 100% at a 10-turn shock to ~18% at 18 turns) — savings delay starvation, they don't abolish it.

Compose / boundaries: **zero added LLM calls and zero RNG** — accumulation is deterministic float state-math, so it can't desync the seeded stream; the draw-down is deterministic too. The default run is byte-identical when the system is off (`storage_on=False`, the default — the loop never calls `storage.accumulate`, the buffer step and the wealth overlay both gate on the flag), and storage is *also* intrinsically gated on settlement, so a v1 nomad never banks regardless. `storage.py` imports only `world` + `strategy` (one-directional), so the world layer stays dependency-free; the renderer/summary wealth line is a pure read printed only when storage is on; god_mode's drought is REUSED, not modified. 105 tests green (99 + 6 new: surplus banks only above need and only when settled; stockpile never exceeds the cap; a starving member with savings survives while one without dies; wealth tracks personality + knowledge and was never assigned; accumulate is zero-LLM/zero-RNG; storage-off run == v1).

The sim now has WEALTH — a bounded personal surplus that emerges from character and knowledge, and that decides who survives a shock. That asymmetry (some agents have more than others, and it is worth something) is exactly the substrate Phase 2's trade (M2.3) needs. Tagged v2.3.

## V2 / M2.3 — Trade, money & proprietary knowledge (Phase 2 CLOSED)

The historical step: M2.2 created WEALTH and inequality — some agents hold far more food/skill than others. M2.3 makes that asymmetry MOVE. Trade exists precisely BECAUSE agents differ (rich/poor, skilled/unskilled, fed/starving); this milestone lets those differences flow through VOLUNTARY exchange at a price that EMERGES from circumstance. It is the moment settlements become an economy, and it closes Phase 2.

Four pieces, all pure state-math (zero added LLM calls, zero RNG of their own):

**Specialization — a second producer skill.** `hunting` joins the tech tree as a sibling of `farming` off the same `tools` prereq, with its own producer function (`knowledge.hunt`) gated identically on KNOWING the skill. It produces food by a distinct mechanic/location — roaming game on a wider ring (Chebyshev 2–3) rather than a tended adjacent plot — so a population now holds two genuinely different producer skills. That skill gap is what gives knowledge-trade something real to exchange: a farmer lacks hunting and vice-versa.

**Money — emergent and food-backed (not fiat).** A new `agent.money` is minted ONLY from food surplus that runs PAST the M2.2 storage cap (`economy.mint`): once an agent's larder is full the food it keeps producing can't be stored, so instead of being wasted it becomes a money claim on that food. Money has value only because it is redeemable as food to survive (`storage.draw_down` now spends stockpile then money) and because others accept it in trade. AUTHORITY-DECREED / MINTED (fiat) currency and WAGE-LABOR are explicitly out of scope, deferred to Phase 3 (stated in code).

**Trade — voluntary exchange at an emergent price.** `economy.trade` is a deterministic pass over adjacent pairs (one best trade per pair per turn) matching food↔money and guarded-knowledge↔money exchanges. The price is the MIDPOINT of the seller's reservation and the buyer's value, so it MOVES with conditions: buyer hunger lifts the food price, seller surplus (larder near the cap) sinks it, and a skill's LOCAL RARITY (how many nearby already know it) plus the buyer's skill-gap set its worth. Both sides end up better off by their own valuation — the price sits strictly between reservation and value — which is exactly what separates trade from theft (untouched).

**Proprietary knowledge — guard vs. give.** `knowledge.guards(agent, item)` decides, FROM PERSONALITY (never assigned), whether a holder withholds a skill from free M1.1 diffusion: an independent/competitive agent guards its valuable skills and sells them; a friendly/curious/cautious agent still teaches free. `diffuse` skips a guarded item (when the economy is on); `trade` is the additional, paid-only path for what a guard won't give away. So the same skill leaks free from generous holders while guarded holders sell it.

**HEADLINE 1 — emergent price.** The SAME skill 'hunting' prices at **7.0** money when rare and the buyer lacks a producer skill, **3.4** when semi-common, **4.0** when the buyer already farms (smaller skill-gap), and **no deal at all (None)** when common enough that the buyer's value falls below the guard's fixed reservation. In two real executed trades it sold for **7.0** in a village where only the seller knew it and **0.0** (no sale) where 3 of 4 neighbours already hunted. Food prices rose monotonically with buyer hunger (1.5 → 3.0 → 4.5 per unit at hunger 3/6/9) and with seller scarcity. A fixed ratio is falsified: price tracks circumstance.

**HEADLINE 2 — trade redistributes by asymmetry.** In the controlled case a rich, unskilled farmer (food+money 35, knows only farming) beside a poor, starving hunter (0 wealth, hunger 8, knows only hunting) traded: the skill flowed skilled→unskilled (the farmer learned hunting), and food/money flowed rich→poor (the hunter ended with a survival buffer) — both better off. At population scale (120 agents, 70 turns) **45 trades fired (31 knowledge, 14 food) and 27 agents bought a producer skill they lacked**. Surplus and skills move from those who have them to those who need them.

The honest judgment (the real result of M2.3): **a real economy emerges** — prices move with scarcity, desperation and surplus; surplus and skills flow to where they're needed; and knowledge is genuinely guarded by some and given by others. Three calibration insights worth recording. (1) **Local rarity must exclude the seller.** The first cut counted the seller among "nearby knowers," so in a small neighbourhood where only the seller knew a skill, rarity computed as 0 ("everyone here knows it") and NO trade ever fired — the opposite of intended. Excluding the prospective seller from the rarity denominator fixed it: a skill only the seller holds nearby is correctly dear. (2) **The guard's reservation must be a FIXED floor, not rarity-scaled.** Initially both buyer-value and seller-reservation scaled linearly with rarity, so their ratio was constant and a deal was NEVER impossible (price just shrank toward zero). Making the reservation a fixed floor means that as a skill spreads its value sinks below the floor and the skill becomes genuinely un-sellable (price None) — that crossing is what makes a common skill un-tradable rather than merely cheap, and it is what stops guarded knowledge from selling forever. (3) **Money is realistically scarce early.** Because money mints only from surplus PAST the cap, it appears only once agents saturate their larders (the richest few), so early exchange is dominated by knowledge-for-food barter and money circulates mainly among the wealthy — an honest consequence of food-backing, not a bug; and a desperate buyer pays a steep premium (a hunger-8 buyer spent 15 money for ~3.8 food), which is the desperation price working as designed.

Compose / boundaries: zero added LLM calls and zero RNG (mint and trade are deterministic over a stable iteration, so they can't desync the seeded stream); the default run is byte-identical when the economy is off (`economy_on=False` — mint/trade never called, diffuse's guard-skip gated off, money ignored in the buffer), and hunting production is a no-op drawing no RNG when nobody is a fed hunter, exactly like farming, so adding the unconditional `hunt()` call and the `hunting` tech-tree entry leaves v1 and every M1.x run byte-identical. `economy.py` imports only world + storage + knowledge (one-directional); the renderer/summary money+skills line is a pure read printed only when the economy is on; god_mode untouched (AST boundary tests green). 113 tests green (105 + 8 new: emergent price varies with conditions; trade mutually beneficial/voluntary; competitive-guarded knowledge doesn't free-diffuse but sells; friendly knowledge still free-diffuses; hunting produces food only for knowers; surplus→money→purchase round-trips and money redeems to survive; mint/trade zero-LLM/zero-RNG; economy-off run == v1).

Phase 2 is CLOSED: settlements that emerged from the food economy now have storable wealth, a food-backed currency, specialization, and voluntary trade at emergent prices — a working economy. The asymmetries are in motion. Tagged v2.2.

## V2 / M3.1 — Wage labor: the first institution (Phase 3 opens)

The historical step: Phases 1–2 built emergent MATERIAL mechanics (knowledge, food, wealth, prices) that mostly EQUILIBRATE — left alone they settle toward a steady state. Phase 3 builds INSTITUTIONS: persistent structures that coordinate and constrain many agents, and the governing rule for all of Phase 3 is that an institution must EMERGE from existing asymmetries, never be installed. M3.1 is the first, and the first DISEQUILIBRATING mechanic: a rich agent EMPLOYS a poor one to produce for it at a wage, and because the employer captures `output − wage` the rich–poor gap COMPOUNDS instead of settling. That compounding is the intended result — it manufactures the class tension later Phase 3 milestones (law, conflict, governance) will respond to. Scope was held strictly to employment relationships + emergent wages + measurable compounding; NO governance/leaders/law/tax/revolt (later Phase 3), NO fiat/minted money (still deferred — wages are paid in the existing food-backed money/stockpile).

The mechanic, all pure state-math (zero added LLM calls, zero RNG of its own; `labor.py`):

**Roles EMERGE from wealth + skill, never assigned.** An EMPLOYER is a settled agent with CAPITAL (`money + stockpile ≥ EMPLOYER_MIN_CAPITAL = 5`) AND a producer skill it can't fully work alone (the means of production) — your rich M2.3 producers. A WORKER is a settled POOR agent (`wealth < WORKER_MAX_WEALTH = 5`) with NO producer skill of its own, for whom a wage beats foraging/starving — your M2.2 have-nots. `is_employer`/`is_worker` are PURE READS of existing state with no flag set anywhere: an agent with no capital can never be an employer (`capacity = wealth // CAPITAL_PER_WORKER`, 0 when broke) and a self-sufficient agent (owns a producer skill, or wealth ≥ `SELF_SUFFICIENT_WEALTH = 15`) never takes a wage. Strip an employer's capital and the role evaporates — verified.

**The relationship PERSISTS — this is what makes it an institution.** A link `{employer, worker, wage, since}` lives in `world_state["employments"]` and SURVIVES across turns until it ends. Each employed turn (`labor.update`): the worker produces `LABOR_OUTPUT = 2.0` for the employer (means + labor → the EMPLOYER's stockpile, overflow past the cap → money under the M2.3 rule), the employer PAYS the worker the wage (`economy._settle`, money first then food), and the worker CONSUMES `COST_OF_LIVING = SUBSISTENCE_WAGE` of subsistence (the wage IS its food — which is why it survives). Net: employer `+(output − wage)`, worker `+(wage − cost-of-living)` ≈ 0 at a subsistence wage. A link ENDS when a party dies, the employer loses its capital/means (lets the worker go), or the worker becomes self-sufficient (gains a skill / climbs past 15 → QUITS, upward mobility — with hysteresis so it isn't hired-and-fired on one threshold).

**The wage EMERGES from the labor market.** `wage = subsistence + leverage × (output − subsistence)`, bounded to `[SUBSISTENCE_WAGE = 1.0, LABOR_OUTPUT = 2.0)`, where `leverage = market_tightness × (1 − desperation)`. Tightness = `openings / (openings + workers)`: a WORKER'S market (scarce labor) → leverage ~1 → wage near output; an EMPLOYER'S market (abundant labor) → leverage ~0 → wage near subsistence. Desperation = `hunger / HUNGER_MAX` discounts a starving worker's leverage. Both an abundant-labor glut AND worker desperation compress the band toward subsistence — emergent EXPLOITATION — but subsistence is POSSIBLE, never automatic: the same work pays differently as conditions change. `wage < output` always (employer profits) and `wage ≥ subsistence` always (worker survives), so the relationship is voluntary and mutually beneficial, just lopsided.

**HEADLINE 1 — compounding inequality (the disequilibrium).** Matched cast (2 rich+skilled employers + 8 poor+unskilled+hungry workers, same start), wage labor ON vs OFF, 20 turns. Wealth Gini with labor ON climbed **0.45 → 0.51 → 0.55 → 0.57 → 0.59** (turns 0/5/10/15/20) — monotone, compounding; with labor OFF it stayed **flat at 0.45** (no exchange, static wealth). By turn 20 the employers had banked ~87 each while every worker sat at ~5.9 (near subsistence). Same agents, same start — the only difference is whether the institution ran. Inequality compounds rather than equilibrating, which is the whole point.

**HEADLINE 2 — emergent wage / exploitation.** The SAME job (output 2.0) priced across the labor market: in a worker's market (1 worker, 8 openings) a real hire formed at wage **1.89** (near output — the worker captures the value); in an employer's market (8 desperate workers, 1 opening) the real hire formed at **1.00 — exactly subsistence** (the employer captures the surplus). The micro table shows the full surface: a fed worker earns 1.95 / 1.50 / 1.05 across scarce / balanced / glut markets, and a starving worker earns 1.00 in EVERY market (desperation crushes leverage to zero). A fixed wage is falsified — subsistence EMERGED from the supply+desperation conditions, it was not hardcoded.

**Mutually entered, not slavery/theft.** Two identical desperate poor workers, capital enough to hire one: the employed worker was fed by its (near-subsistence) wage and SURVIVED (and even accumulated ~0.9), while the identical UNEMPLOYED one starved to death. Even when exploited the worker still gains NET — its life — which is exactly what separates emergent exploitation from slavery/theft.

The honest judgment (the real result of M3.1): **the mechanic is sound and all three claims hold deterministically** — roles fall out of existing wealth/skill with no flag, the wage genuinely moves with the labor market (reaching subsistence under glut+desperation without being scripted there), and inequality measurably compounds while the institution runs. The instructive finding is about ORGANIC PERSISTENCE in the full loop. Running the live simulation (40 procedurally-generated agents, settlements + economy + tech-tree, farming seeded), wage labor **DID emerge organically during the transient** — at turn 40 there were 11 employers, an unskilled worker, and 2 active employment links, all falling out of real `world_state` with nothing assigned. But by turn 80+ it had DISSOLVED: free M1.1 knowledge diffusion taught farming to all 38 survivors, so everyone became skilled-and-rich, the unskilled underclass vanished, and with no one lacking the means of production there was no one to hire. This is not a bug in M3.1 — it is an honest interplay finding: **wage labor requires a persistent inequality of the MEANS of production, and free knowledge diffusion competes that inequality away.** Persistent organic wage labor therefore depends on the upstream asymmetry persisting — proprietary/guarded knowledge (M2.3) keeping skills scarce, or capital concentration — which is why the deterministic verification (like every M2.x verify before it) constructs the settled rich-skilled/poor-unskilled class structure directly rather than waiting on a long offline run to manufacture and then preserve it. The class engine is built and works; keeping a working class in existence over long horizons is a tuning/Phase-3 question (it is exactly the tension law and governance will arbitrate), not a defect of the wage mechanic.

Compose / boundaries: zero added LLM calls and zero RNG (the labor market is deterministic state-math over a stable iteration — employers matched to nearest available workers with sorted tie-breaks — so it can't desync the seeded stream); the default run is byte-identical when wage labor is off (`labor_on=False`, the default — `labor.update` is never called and no links form; the summary employment overlay gates on the flag). Employment links live ONLY in `world_state` (no new `Agent` field), so an off run is structurally unchanged. `labor.py` imports only world + storage + economy (one-directional), keeping the world layer dependency-free; `--labor` implies `--economy` (and so settlements + storage), runs AFTER trade/mint so this turn's wealth/skill drives who employs whom; the renderer/summary employment line is a pure read printed only when labor is on; god_mode untouched (AST boundary tests green). **123 tests green** (113 + 10 new: wage varies with supply+desperation (not fixed); under glut+desperation wage reaches subsistence but never below the survival floor; output flows to the employer and wage to the worker; an agent without capital never employs; roles emerge from wealth+skill and aren't assigned; the link persists across turns and is mutually entered; worker quits when self-sufficient / broke employer lets go; inequality compounds with labor on vs flat off; labor is zero-LLM/zero-RNG; labor-off run == v1).

Phase 3 is OPEN: the first institution exists. Wage labor emerges from the M2.2/M2.3 class structure, its price moves with the labor market down to emergent exploitation, and it makes inequality COMPOUND instead of equilibrate — the disequilibrium and class engine the rest of Phase 3 (law, conflict, governance) will run on.

## V2 / M3.2 — Legitimate leadership: authority by trust (Phase 3)

The historical step: M3.1 built the first institution (wage labor) — political-economic power DOWNSTREAM of wealth, the rich employing the poor and inequality compounding. M3.2 builds the first POLITICAL institution and, crucially, the first power that is NOT downstream of wealth: a LEADER legitimated by TRUST. The most-trusted agent need not be the richest, so political power DECOUPLES from economic power — the tension later Phase 3 milestones (law, taxation, conflict) will turn on. Scope was held strictly to emergence of a legitimate leader from the existing trust network + one minimal real effect + loss of legitimacy; NO taxation, NO law/legislation, NO revolt (later Phase 3). A leader in M3.2 has INFLUENCE, not the power to tax or legislate.

The mechanic is a PURE READ of the EXISTING v1 trust system — `leadership.py` writes no trust values, makes zero LLM calls, and draws zero RNG; it reads `agent.relationships` and its own `world_state["leaders"]` record:

**Leadership EMERGES from a cohered trust cluster, never a global-max lookup.** A leader emerges within a SETTLEMENT only when a real FOLLOWING coheres: a candidate needs `≥ MIN_FOLLOWERS = 2` co-settlers each trusting it `≥ FORM_TRUST` (tied to `trust.HIGH_THRESHOLD = 2`, so it reads exactly as the v1 trust system's own "high"). The leader is the CENTRE of the cluster — the candidate the most co-settlers trust (most followers, alphabetical tie-break) — stored as `world_state["leaders"][sid] = {leader, followers, since}`. The single most-trusted agent with ONE ardent admirer does NOT clear the bar — authority requires a CLUSTER, not a high score — and a fractured, low-trust settlement where no candidate reaches the bar has NO leader at all (correct: not every group has one; we do not force one).

**Legitimacy is EARNED and CONTINGENT — this is what makes it legitimacy, not a crown.** Leadership persists only while the following persists. HYSTERESIS (a Schmitt trigger on trust) stops it flickering on a single-turn wobble: a following FORMS on the high bar (`FORM_TRUST = 2`) but an incumbent is RETAINED on a lower bar (`KEEP_TRUST = 1`), so a follower drifting `2 → 1` does not unseat anyone, while real erosion (trust falling to `0` or negative — e.g. the leader turning hostile, a `−3` hit) drops the follower below the keep bar and can end the role. A NEW, strictly-more-trusted centre DISPLACES the incumbent when the following shifts (its form-following bigger than the incumbent's), resetting `since`. If a leader's retained following falls below `MIN_FOLLOWERS` and no one else qualifies, the leadership DISSOLVES.

**The minimal real effect is COORDINATING INFLUENCE — never tax/law.** A leader coordinates its following: a follower coheres MORE TIGHTLY around its leader than a mere settler does around the settlement centre. ## V2 / M3.3 — Taxation & redistribution: legitimacy acts on wealth (Phase 3)

The historical step: M3.1 built the CLASS ENGINE (wage labor — the rich employer captures the worker's surplus and inequality COMPOUNDS, a disequilibrating spiral). M3.2 built the LEGITIMACY ENGINE (a leader legitimated by TRUST, a political power DECOUPLED from wealth). M3.3 is their COLLISION — the first force that BENDS the M3.1 spiral: a legitimate leader TAXES its wealthy followers and REDISTRIBUTES to its poor ones. Political legitimacy acquires the power to act on economic inequality. Scope was held strictly to taxation + redistribution + a legitimacy backlash that self-limits over-taxation; NO law/legislation, NO revolt (later Phase 3), NO fiat money (taxes move the existing food-backed money/stockpile, conserving total wealth — redistribution, not minting).

The mechanic is deterministic state-math (`taxation.py`, zero LLM, zero RNG) that COMPOSES the two engines and, uniquely for Phase 3, WRITES trust (M3.2 only read it):

**Taxation requires LEGITIMACY (this ties the two engines).** ONLY a settlement with a legitimate M3.2 leader can tax — the power is downstream of legitimacy, not of wealth or a flag. The leader taxes its FOLLOWERS (the M3.2 trust cluster), never non-followers, never itself: a follower whose wealth exceeds `RICH_THRESHOLD = 10` is taxed `tax_rate` of the EXCESS above it (progressive) into a pool; the pool is redistributed to followers below `POOR_THRESHOLD = 5`, weighted by NEED (the poorest receive the most). Taxation only fires when there is BOTH a tax base and a redistribution target (it exists to redistribute). Total wealth is CONSERVED (pool taken == pool given to the cent).

**Redistribution BENDS inequality (the headline).** Matched led settlements, the M3.1 labor spiral (2 rich employers + 7 poor hungry workers, an EMPLOYER'S market -> subsistence wages -> employers compound) running in BOTH arms, taxation the only difference. Within-settlement wealth Gini over 20 turns: **taxation OFF stays HIGH — 0.665 -> 0.608 (the spiral persists); taxation ON is bent down and held LOW — 0.665 -> 0.293 -> 0.460, every turn at or below the untaxed arm, ending 0.148 lower.** The ON curve drifting back up from 0.293 to 0.460 is the dynamic tension made visible: redistribution fights the labor spiral each turn, holding inequality far below where it would otherwise sit. Political legitimacy measurably checks the M3.1 spiral — the milestone's whole point.

**Legitimacy BACKLASH self-limits taxation (emergent consent of the governed).** Taxation costs the leader trust — but only OVERREACH does. Up to `CONSENT_RATE = 0.35` the governed accept taxation as legitimate (consent), so a moderate levy draws NO resentment; above it each taxed follower withdraws `round((rate − CONSENT_RATE) × RESENT_SCALE)` trust per turn. Redistribution meanwhile EARNS the leader `+GRATITUDE` from each poor follower it lifts. The SAME leader, two rates, two fates: **MODERATE (30%, within consent) — the taxed-rich trust holds at 2 (no resentment), the poor's trust rises to 3 (gratitude), the leader is SUSTAINED indefinitely; OVER (90%) — resentment of −2/turn crashes the rich below M3.2's `KEEP_TRUST`, the following collapses below `MIN_FOLLOWERS`, and next turn M3.2's contingency STRIPS the leadership (and with it the power to tax — taxing stops).** Tyranny is punished by withdrawal of consent, emergent, not scripted. This is the FIRST Phase-3 institution that writes trust, and it writes through the existing `trust.adjust_trust` (the logged, grudge-aware path conversations use), so the backlash is a first-class part of the trust network the leadership engine reads — the two engines are genuinely wired together, not bolted side by side.

**FLOWS are correct.** A constructed cast (leader 8, Rich 50, Poor1 1, Poor2 3, Middle 7, Outsider 99-but-not-a-follower): after one levy at 30%, Rich 50->38 (taxed on the 40 excess), Poor1 1->9 and Poor2 3->7 (the POOREST lifted MOST, need-weighted), and the leader, the mid-wealth follower, and the non-follower outsider are ALL untouched; total wealth conserved 168.0 -> 168.0.

The honest judgment (the real result of M3.3): **redistribution measurably BENDS the inequality curve, and the backlash makes over-taxation genuinely SELF-LIMITING — moderate taxation is sustained by consent while tyranny is punished by trust loss that fires M3.2's contingency.** No piece is mis-tuned: the consent band (taxation below `CONSENT_RATE` drawing zero resentment) is what lets moderate taxation persist where a naive per-turn trust cost would erode any leader who ever taxed; and because the rich are taxed on the EXCESS above a threshold, the levy is progressive rather than a flat head-tax. Two honest caveats for the record. (1) The headline OFF arm is engineered to be an EMPLOYER'S market (workers held hungry/at subsistence — the M3.1 treadmill) so the spiral PERSISTS and the contrast is clean; in a WORKER'S market (scarce labor, high wages) labor itself compresses inequality and both arms fall, though taxation ON still ends materially lower — so taxation reliably *reduces* inequality, but how dramatic the bend looks depends on the underlying labor market. (2) In fully ORGANIC end-to-end runs (`--labor --leadership --taxation`, seeds 3/7) leadership emerges from built trust and the leader DOES tax and redistribute, but the pools are tiny (~0.0–0.2) because organic wealth stays modest — few followers cross `RICH_THRESHOLD`. The strong-inequality demonstration is therefore the constructed verify, which is honest about being constructed; the organic run proves the institution COMPOSES and fires, not that organic agents spontaneously generate dynastic wealth (they don't, yet). Both are reported.

Compose / boundaries: zero added LLM calls and zero RNG (taxation is deterministic state-math over sorted settlement ids and sorted follower names; verified RNG-untouched and `{decision:0, strategy:0}` across 19 passes), and it CONSERVES total wealth (verified byte-equal before/after). It WRITES trust — by design, the milestone's point — but only through `trust.adjust_trust`, the same logged path conversations use, so the writes are first-class and inspectable. The default run is byte-identical when taxation is off (`taxation_on=False`, the default — `taxation.update` is never called). Taxation rides `world_state` (`taxation_on`, `tax_rate`, plus the M3.2 leader record) with no new `Agent` field. `taxation.py` imports only world + storage + trust (one-directional). `--taxation` implies `--leadership` (and so `--settlements`) — a leader is needed to levy — but NOT `--labor` (taxation acts on whatever wealth exists; pair with `--labor` for the spiral to bend); runs AFTER `leadership.update` so it taxes with this turn's leader and the trust it writes feeds next turn's leadership re-evaluation (the backlash loop); god_mode untouched (AST boundary tests green); levy events mirrored into the chronological `world_state["events"]` log. **135 tests green** (130 + 5 new: only a legitimate leader can tax (no leader -> no redistribution); redistribution lowers within-settlement Gini vs an untaxed run; over-taxation costs legitimacy while moderate is sustained; flows go rich->poor among followers and conserve wealth; taxation-off run == v1).

The first REDISTRIBUTIVE institution exists: political legitimacy can now check economic inequality, and the check is bounded by the consent of the governed — over-reach dissolves the very legitimacy that enables it. The two Phase-3 engines (class and legitimacy) are now coupled: wealth still compounds (M3.1), trust still confers contingent authority (M3.2), and that authority can now bend the wealth curve (M3.3) only so far as the governed permit. Law, taxation-of-trade, and conflict are the next collisions. Tagged v3.3.

## V2 / M3.4 — Conquest & monarchy: power seized by force (Phase 3)

The historical step: M3.2 built authority by CONSENT (trust-legitimacy, power granted from below). M3.4 builds the OTHER source of power: DOMINATION — power SEIZED from above by FORCE. A wealthy agent converts money into an ARMY of real fighters and attacks a settlement to seize it, becoming MONARCH — a persistent ruling title held by force, not consent. This completes the class engine's arc: M3.1 wealth COMPOUNDS → M3.2/M3.3 legitimacy RESISTS and REDISTRIBUTES → M3.4 wealth ESCALATES to violence and takes the crown anyway. Scope was held strictly to conflict (force vs force), conquest of ONE settlement, the MONARCH institution (rule by force, losable), and the force-vs-legitimacy collision; NO underclass revolt, NO inter-kingdom war/diplomacy, NO multi-settlement kingdoms (all later).

The mechanic (`monarchy.py`, zero LLM, **zero RNG** — fully deterministic so a fight can never desync the seeded stream) COMPOSES M3.1 wealth, M3.2 loyalty, and the death machinery:

**Military power EMERGES from wealth via REAL fighters (never a wealth compare).** An ASPIRANT (wealth ≥ `MIN_WAR_CHEST`=10) raises a FORCE by SPENDING money to hire FIGHTERS — real, poor/desperate agents (wealth < `MERC_MAX_WEALTH`=5, echoing M3.1's labor pool) within `MUSTER_RADIUS`, paying each `FIGHTER_COST`=5 (money then food). Attacker force = the NUMBER of fighters actually mustered, capped by `war_chest // FIGHTER_COST` AND by how many mercenaries are in range. So power runs wealth → soldiers → force, with soldiers being real agents: a broke aspirant musters nobody and conquers nothing.

**A fight turns on MUSTERED FORCE, not on wealth.** Attacker force is resolved against the settlement's DEFENCE: a MONARCH's standing GARRISON, else a legitimate M3.2 LEADER's loyal FOLLOWERS (loyalty becomes defensive force — the collision), else the militia of living MEMBERS. The attacker wins iff `attacker_force > defender_force` (strict — a tie is held by the seat; the attacker must OVERCOME the defence). War is COSTLY: each side loses `round(CASUALTY_RATE=0.5 × opposing_force)` fighters (capped at its own size) — real agents who DIE through `population.announce_death` (a battle event, survivor memories, a queued respawn, exactly like starvation). Figureheads (the aspirant, the defending leader/monarch) are not slain — they win or lose the TITLE; the rank-and-file pay in blood, so a defended conquest thins the leader's following (feeding M3.2 next turn) and a failed assault burns the aspirant's army.

**MONARCH — the institution (persistent, rule-by-force, LOSABLE).** The victor becomes monarch: `world_state["monarchs"][sid] = {monarch, since, garrison}`, the garrison being the surviving army (a standing force that defends the crown). A monarch rules by FORCE: it LEVIES `MONARCH_LEVY_RATE`=0.20 of each member's wealth above a threshold into its OWN coffers, WITHOUT consent. The crown is LOSABLE — a stronger later aspirant attacks the garrison with the same mechanic and OVERTHROWS the monarch (succession reset; never permanent). **The sharp contrast with M3.3:** M3.3 taxation needs a legitimate leader and the CONSENT of the governed, is REDISTRIBUTIVE (rich followers → poor followers), conserves wealth, and SELF-LIMITS through a trust backlash; a monarch's levy needs NEITHER a leader NOR consent, is EXTRACTIVE (members → the crown), and has no trust check — domination, not government.

**Monarch vs trust-leader: two roles, two sources, force takes precedence.** Conquest does NOT erase the `leaders[]` record — the trust-leader may still be loved (consent survives occupation) — but the monarch overrides governance: it is the monarch, not the consent-leader, who rules and levies the seized settlement. FORCE > CONSENT for control; the dark point of M3.4 is exactly that a trusted, legitimate leader can be conquered by a bought army and reduced to a powerless figurehead under a crown it never granted (verified: after an overwhelming assault, `monarchs[S001]=Rich` while `leaders[S001]=Chief` coexist).

**HEADLINE 1 — wealth → force → crown.** A wealthy aspirant (Rich, wealth 30 → could fund 6 fighters) musters 4 REAL fighters (only 4 mercenaries were in range), spending wealth 30 → 10, and seizes an unled 2-member town (4 > 2 militia) to become monarch; a BROKE aspirant (wealth 3 < the war-chest floor) musters 0 fighters and takes nothing. The crown is bought with real soldiers funded by wealth — not a number compare.

**HEADLINE 2 — force vs legitimacy collides, BOTH outcomes.** Against Chief's 4 loyal followers: the WEALTHIEST agent in the world (Rich, wealth 200) who can muster only 3 fighters is REPELLED (3 < 4 — loyalty repels the richer-but-smaller force); the same Rich mustering 7 fighters WINS (7 > 4 — overwhelming bought force overcomes loyalty). The outcome turns on mustered force, not wealth: a real fight, not a foregone wealth-max.

The honest judgment (the real result of M3.4): **monarchy genuinely EMERGES from wealth-funded force — not a wealth-max lookup — force-vs-legitimacy genuinely COLLIDES (loyalty can repel, overwhelming wealth wins), and the crown is genuinely CONTESTED (losable to a stronger army).** No piece is a foregone conclusion: because force = the count of *real* mustered fighters (gated by both war chest and merc availability) and defence = the count of *loyal* defenders, the richest agent loses when it cannot field enough soldiers — wealth is necessary but not sufficient. One deliberate design choice worth recording: the resolution is DETERMINISTIC on the force counts (strict `>`, defender holds ties) rather than a seeded coin-flip — I chose reproducible determinism over hidden RNG (the prompt allowed either) because it keeps the seeded stream un-desynced and makes the collision legible; "favours the stronger" is honoured, and the non-determinism-on-wealth comes from loyalty being an independent input, not from a die. Two honest caveats. (1) The LOOP launches only WINNABLE assaults — a `fieldable_force > defenders` guard, added after an unguarded first cut had aspirants spamming doomed 2-fighter attacks every turn and bleeding the population for nothing; a rational commander raises an army to win, so an aspirant who cannot out-muster the defence bides its time. This is a LOOP policy only — `attempt_conquest` itself resolves any matchup, so the verify can still stage a doomed assault and watch loyalty repel it. (2) With that guard, fully ORGANIC `--labor --leadership --taxation --monarchy` runs produce NO successful conquest (and, correctly, no doomed spam): organic agents never concentrate enough wealth to field an army larger than a village's loyal core — the same limitation as M3.1's transient wage labor and M3.3's tiny organic pools. This is a genuinely meaningful finding, not a gap: in an egalitarian society nobody can buy a big enough army to conquer — which is precisely why the verify must construct the tyrant to show what happens when someone can.

Compose / boundaries: zero added LLM calls and zero RNG (deterministic iteration over sorted settlement ids, aspirants by wealth then name, mercenaries by distance then name, casualties by name — verified RNG-untouched and `{decision:0, strategy:0}`, and the battle trajectory byte-identical on re-run). The default run is byte-identical when monarchy is off (`monarchy_on=False`, the default — `monarchy.update` is never called, no armies/crowns/battle deaths form; the summary monarch overlay gates on the flag). Records live only in `world_state["monarchs"]` (no new `Agent` field). `monarchy.py` imports world + population + economy (one-directional). `--monarchy` implies `--settlements` only (a town to seize) — NOT the economy or leadership (pair with `--economy`/`--labor` for army-funding wealth and `--leadership` for the loyalty to collide with). The SECOND source of power exists: domination by force, alongside M3.2's authority by consent. The class engine reaches its dark climax — wealth, having compounded (M3.1) and been resisted and redistributed by legitimacy (M3.2/M3.3), now escalates to violence and buys the crown outright (M3.4) — yet even the crown is not safe, contestable by the next, larger army. ## V2 / M3.5 — Kingdoms & vassalage: feudalism (Phase 3)

The historical step: M3.4 built a MONARCH who seizes ONE settlement by force. M3.5 is the SCALE-UP — a monarch conquers NEIGHBOURING settlements into a multi-settlement KINGDOM, a two-level FEUDAL HIERARCHY (KING → VASSAL LORDS → their settlements). The model is faithful feudalism: the vassal bargain is TRIBUTE + military SERVICE flowing UP, PROTECTION + local AUTONOMY flowing DOWN, and loyalty is CONDITIONAL — it erodes under a grasping overlord and a pushed vassal can BREAK AWAY. That conditional loyalty makes royal power CONTINGENT (on keeping vassals loyal), not absolute. Scope was held strictly to formation-by-conquest, the vassal hierarchy, the tribute/service bargain, conditional loyalty, and BREAKAWAY (a vassal leaving the realm); baronial civil war, defection to a RIVAL king (needs M3.6), and rebellion cascades are deferred and stated as boundaries in `kingdoms.py`.

The mechanic (`kingdoms.py`, zero LLM, **zero RNG**) COMPOSES M3.4 conquest, M3.2 trust, and M3.3's backlash shape — and to do it without duplicating the fight, M3.4's `attempt_conquest` was refactored to call a new shared `monarchy.resolve_battle` (the identical strict-`>` resolution + `CASUALTY_RATE` deaths), which M3.5 reuses verbatim, so both milestones fight by the same maths (M3.4 verified byte-identical after the refactor):

**Formation by conquest → vassalage (HEADLINE 1).** A monarch's `conquer_neighbour` aims the M3.4 fight OUTWARD at an adjacent settlement (within `KINGDOM_REACH`=8). On victory the conquered settlement joins the realm and KEEPS its local ruler — its M3.2 trust-leader or M3.4 local monarch — who swears FEALTY and rules on as a VASSAL LORD (the local `leaders[]`/`monarchs[]` record is NOT erased: local autonomy preserved). A ruler-less town is held DIRECTLY (king installed as its monarch). A hopeless defence (outnumbered ≥ `SUBMIT_RATIO`=3) SUBMITS without bloodshed. Verified: King (monarch of S001) conquers trust-led S002 then S003, Chief2/Chief3 become vassals, `kingdoms[King]` shows a two-level realm of 3 settlements with both local leaderships intact.

**The feudal bargain — tribute up, service up, autonomy/protection down (HEADLINE 2).** TRIBUTE cascades UP the levels (`tribute`): a vassal levies `TRIBUTE_RATE`=0.20 of its settlement members' wealth-above-threshold (members → vassal), then a `KING_SHARE` of THAT fresh take flows up to the king (vassal → king) — verified across both levels (members 40.0→34.0, vassal 10.0→14.5, king 10.0→11.5) and CONSERVING total wealth (60.0 == 60.0): a realm concentrates wealth at the crown *through* its vassals. MILITARY SERVICE (`muster_realm`): the king's host = its own bought fighters PLUS every LOYAL vassal's mustered contingent — realm strength = the SUM of loyal vassals' forces (verified: the host includes the vassal's fighters; after breakaway it does not).

**Conditional loyalty — the generative feudal tension (HEADLINE 3).** A vassal's loyalty IS its trust in the king (reusing the v1 trust system; fealty seeds it to `LOYAL_TRUST`=2). Heavy royal demands ERODE it through the EXACT M3.3 tax-backlash shape (now aimed at the crown): when the king's share exceeds `KING_CONSENT`=0.35 each vassal withdraws `round((rate − KING_CONSENT) × RESENT_SCALE)` trust per turn. Verified — same vassal, two treatments, two fates: a grasping king (share 0.9) drives Chief's trust 2 → 0 → −2 → break-away on turn 3 (NOT on the first hard turn — the patience counter is the hysteresis; recovered loyalty resets it), while a fair king (share 0.25, within consent) draws no resentment and holds the vassal indefinitely. Royal power is contingent on not over-grasping — feudal tyranny self-limits exactly as M3.3's civic tyranny did.

The honest judgment (the real result of M3.5): **kingdoms genuinely EMERGE as faithful feudal hierarchies — conquest → vassalage (local ruler kept), tribute cascading up across real levels and conserved, loyal vassals serving militarily, the map consolidating — and loyalty is genuinely CONDITIONAL: a pushed vassal breaks away, a fair king holds his realm, neither scripted.** No piece is a foregone conclusion: breakaway turns on accumulated trust (an independent input), not on a flag, and a fair crown provably keeps what a grasping one loses.
